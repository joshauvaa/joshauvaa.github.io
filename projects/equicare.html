Executive Summary

Challenge: Design an HRIS enhancement capable of identifying pay disparities across a global retail organization while balancing HR operations, legal compliance, security, and scalability.

Role: Requirements Analyst responsible for stakeholder analysis, product requirements, backlog definition, and translating business goals into implementation-ready specifications.

1

Discover

Business Problem · Market · Goal

Problem Statement

Global Retail United (GRU), a global retail organization with over 4,000 stores across 40+ countries, manages compensation through a manual, decentralized system — each department uses its own methodology for setting and reviewing pay. That decentralization creates real risk: pay disparities by gender or race can exist and go unnoticed, exposing GRU to legal liability and reputational harm, while giving HR and legal teams no reliable way to detect or report on the problem.

Our task was to design an update to GRU's existing HRIS (modeled on SAP SuccessFactors) that could integrate the organization's other enterprise systems, surface pay disparities with real-time data, and give HR and legal teams the tools to act on what they found.

Market Research

We evaluated existing HRIS and pay-equity solutions to understand common capabilities and identify opportunities for improvement. While the original research informed our recommendations, the supporting documentation is no longer available, so this case study focuses on the resulting product decisions and planning artifacts.

The Goal

Give GRU's HR and legal teams real-time visibility into pay disparities across the organization, replacing department-by-department guesswork with a single, centralized source of truth to highlight them before they became legal/reputational liabilities.

Our team reasoned that a centralized system with role-based access, real-time reporting, and standardized compensation criteria would address the organization's biggest operational and compliance risks while minimizing implementation complexity.

Stakeholders · Scope · Requirements

Stakeholder Analysis

Because compensation decisions affect multiple parts of the organization, one of our first priorities was identifying the stakeholders who would rely on—or be affected by—the system.

Stakeholder Current Issue Needed Benefit
VP of HR Decentralized system, potential pay disparities Centralized system, real-time reports, transparency
Legal Team (CLO & HLC) Potential discrimination liability Objective compensation criteria, flexible reporting
CIO Decentralized IT systems Secure, scalable, integrated platform
HR Managers Lack of transparency in compensation Real-time data, automated processes
Payroll Manager Ever-changing payroll law, no flexible reporting Automated compliance-aware reporting

Scope

We deliberately scoped this as an upgrade to GRU's existing HRIS rather than a full replacement — both to control cost (industry research suggested upgrades can save over 25% versus full migration) and to avoid the risk of a ground-up system migration at this scale. In scope: a centralized system, real-time reporting, objective compensation criteria, performance management modules, and role-based security. Explicitly out of scope: third-party integrations outside GRU, policy authorship, and employee training program design.

Non-Functional Requirements

Because this system would handle highly sensitive compensation and demographic data at scale, the non-functional requirements carried as much weight as the features themselves.

Category Requirement
Scalability Support up to 500,000 concurrent users; accommodate 15% annual growth
Availability 99.9% uptime ("three nines"); ≤8.77 hours planned downtime per year
Performance <2 second response time regardless of client or device
Interoperability Built in Java; integrates with Microsoft Dynamics 365 and Empower via API; supports CSV import/export

Security & Role-Based Access

Centralizing sensitive data (salaries, SSNs, demographic information) into one platform meant access control couldn't be an afterthought. We mapped permissions explicitly by role rather than relying on a generic "admin vs. user" split:

Permission VP of HR HR Manager Payroll Mgr Employee
View personal info (name, salary, SSN) Yes Yes Yes Own only
Edit payroll information No No Yes No
Approve employee requests Yes Yes No No

This table also directly shaped the wireframes below — the Pay screen, for example, explicitly restricts payroll-sheet editing to Payroll Managers, with HR Managers granted only limited edit permissions.

Wireframes · Agile Backlog

Wireframes

We focused on three high-value experiences that demonstrated the product's core functionality and stakeholder priorities. Our wireframes was annotated with interaction logic to communicate implementation intent.

Home Dashboard
Role-personalized announcements, awaiting actions, and upcoming dates with direct links to source tasks.
Comparison Tool
The centerpiece of EquiCare. Built directly against User Story 19 ("As a manager, I want to compare similarly leveled employees and know the percentage difference in pay"). Surfaces an Employee Score, pay trend over time, and an explicit calculated disparity ("Employee 2 is making 30% less than the average manager").
Pay Screen
Role-gated payroll sheet with CSV import/export and a role-distribution breakdown, reflecting the access control table above directly in the UI.

Rather than a polished UI, these mockups served as communication tools translating requirements into concrete user experiences developers could understand and implement.

Agile Process & Backlog

EquiCare's requirements were broken down using a standard three-tier Agile hierarchy, (Epics → Features → User Stories) all tracked in Azure Boards. The backlog held 30+ work items with full traceability from high-level business goals down to testable acceptance criteria, and a portion of that backlog was pulled into a defined Sprint 1.

In practice, this was run as a loosely sprinted workflow rather than strict, by-the-book Scrum. No formal sprint review/retro ceremonies tracked in the tool, and iteration dates weren't always set. The backlog structure and story-writing were emphasized.

Backlog Hierarchy

Epics decomposed into Features, and further into individually testable User Stories. Ex:

Epic Feature User Story
Ensure Integration With Existing Systems Offer Single Sign-On Support (SSO) #5 — HR Manager: As an HR manager, I would like to be able to access different systems at the same time without having to log in multiple times.
Ensure Integration With Existing Systems Develop APIs for system to communicate with other systems #6 — Payroll Manager: As a payroll manager, I would like to integrate a payroll processing API to our HRIS to automate data transfer and reduce human error.
Document Employee Learning Performance Improvement Recommendation #11 - As an employee of GRU, I would like to see how far my progress is in completing the learning material, so I can know much I have left.

Deliver

Rollout · Closing

Rollout & Adoption

Because GRU employees were already familiar with most of the underlying systems being integrated, we designed the rollout to minimize disruption rather than retrain from scratch. In-app tutorials would target new functionality — performance management modules for managers, compensation-criteria tools for HR, and new reporting tools for the Payroll Manager — rather than re-teaching the whole system. The update would also be announced well ahead of launch, with a support team and documentation in place at go-live.

Closing

Although EquiCare concluded during the planning and design phase, it reinforced how much product management happens before development begins. Translating an ambiguous business problem into clear stakeholder needs, prioritized requirements, and implementation-ready documentation was tedious. But understanably so considering all of the nuances behind the products of the age.

The project strengthened my appreciation for the planning work that enables successful execution. Software development can be an absolute mess without clear requirements, thoughtful prioritization, and well-defined constraints.
