EquiCare

A requirements-driven HRIS update designed to surface and reduce pay disparities across a global retail organization — built through stakeholder analysis, compliance research, and detailed wireframing rather than a tested live product.

How to read this case study Unlike DawgRide, this project never reached a built, user-tested product — it's a requirements and systems analysis exercise for a fictional retail organization ("Global Retail United"). The strength here is in stakeholder analysis, a genuinely disciplined Agile backlog (30+ epics/features/stories with real acceptance criteria), and translating abstract requirements into concrete wireframes — not in iterative user testing. 1

Discover

Business Problem · Market · Goal

The Business Problem

Global Retail United (GRU), a global retail organization with over 4,000 stores across 40+ countries, manages compensation through a manual, decentralized system — each department uses its own methodology for setting and reviewing pay. That decentralization creates real risk: pay disparities by gender or race can exist and go unnoticed, exposing GRU to legal liability and reputational harm, while giving HR and legal teams no reliable way to detect or report on the problem.

Our task was to design an update to GRU's existing HRIS (modeled on SAP SuccessFactors) that could integrate the organization's other enterprise systems, surface pay disparities with real-time data, and give HR and legal teams the tools to act on what they found.

The Market

The team did look at existing pay-equity and HRIS tooling as part of this project, but that research is a couple of years old now and I no longer have the original notes or sources to cite accurately. Rather than reconstruct it from memory and risk misattributing specific tools or claims, I'm naming the gap directly — the research happened, but I can't stand behind the specifics anymore.

The Goal

Give GRU's HR and legal teams real-time visibility into pay disparities across the organization, replacing department-by-department guesswork with a single, centralized source of truth — so disparities could be caught and addressed before they became legal or reputational liabilities, rather than discovered after the fact.

Stakeholders · Scope · Requirements

Stakeholder Analysis

Six distinct stakeholder roles were identified, each with different needs and different relationships to the pay equity problem — from the VP of HR needing visibility, to the Chief Security Officer needing to protect the sensitive data the system would now centralize.

Stakeholder Current Issue Needed Benefit
VP of HR Decentralized system, potential pay disparities Centralized system, real-time reports, transparency
Legal Team (CLO & HLC) Potential discrimination liability Objective compensation criteria, flexible reporting
CIO Decentralized IT systems Secure, scalable, integrated platform
HR Managers Lack of transparency in compensation Real-time data, automated processes
Payroll Manager Ever-changing payroll law, no flexible reporting Automated compliance-aware reporting

Scope

We deliberately scoped this as an upgrade to GRU's existing HRIS rather than a full replacement — both to control cost (industry research suggested upgrades can save over 25% versus full migration) and to avoid the risk of a ground-up system migration at this scale. In scope: a centralized system, real-time reporting, objective compensation criteria, performance management modules, and role-based security. Explicitly out of scope: third-party integrations outside GRU, policy authorship, and employee training program design.

Non-Functional Requirements

Because this system would handle highly sensitive compensation and demographic data at scale, the non-functional requirements carried as much weight as the features themselves.

Category Requirement
Scalability Support up to 500,000 concurrent users; accommodate 15% annual growth
Availability 99.9% uptime ("three nines"); ≤8.77 hours planned downtime per year
Performance <2 second response time regardless of client or device
Interoperability Built in Java; integrates with Microsoft Dynamics 365 and Empower via API; supports CSV import/export

Security & Role-Based Access

Centralizing sensitive data (salaries, SSNs, demographic information) into one platform meant access control couldn't be an afterthought. We mapped permissions explicitly by role rather than relying on a generic "admin vs. user" split:

Permission VP of HR HR Manager Payroll Mgr Employee
View personal info (name, salary, SSN) Yes Yes Yes Own only
Edit payroll information No No Yes No
Approve employee requests Yes Yes No No

This table also directly shaped the wireframes below — the Pay screen, for example, explicitly restricts payroll-sheet editing to Payroll Managers, with HR Managers granted only limited edit permissions.

Wireframes · Agile Backlog

Wireframes

Three core screens were designed in Axure, each annotated with interaction logic rather than left as static mockups. Click any image to view it full size.

Home Dashboard
Role-personalized announcements, awaiting actions, and upcoming dates with direct links to source tasks.
Comparison Tool
The centerpiece of EquiCare. Built directly against User Story 19 ("As a manager, I want to compare similarly leveled employees and know the percentage difference in pay"). Surfaces an Employee Score, pay trend over time, and an explicit calculated disparity ("Employee 2 is making 30% less than the average manager").
Pay Screen
Role-gated payroll sheet with CSV import/export and a role-distribution breakdown, reflecting the access control table above directly in the UI.

The Comparison Tool is the strongest artifact in this project because it's the one place where the abstract goal — "detect pay disparity" — becomes a concrete, usable interface. Rather than just reporting raw numbers, it does the interpretive work for the user: calculating and stating the percentage gap against role averages, so a manager doesn't have to do that math themselves to notice a problem.

Agile Process & Backlog

EquiCare's requirements were broken down using a standard three-tier Agile hierarchy: Epics → Features → User Stories, all tracked in Azure Boards. This wasn't loosely structured — the backlog held 30+ work items with full traceability from high-level business goals down to testable acceptance criteria, and a portion of that backlog was pulled into a defined Sprint 1.

To be precise about process maturity: this was run as a loosely sprinted workflow rather than strict, by-the-book Scrum — there were no formal sprint review/retro ceremonies tracked in the tool, and iteration dates weren't always set. The backlog structure and story-writing discipline were real; the ceremony cadence was informal.

Backlog Hierarchy

Each Epic decomposed into Features, which then decomposed into individually testable User Stories. For example:

Epic Feature User Story
Ensure Integration With Existing Systems Offer Single Sign-On Support (SSO) #5 — HR Manager: As an HR manager, I would like to be able to access different systems at the same time without having to log in multiple times.
Ensure Integration With Existing Systems Develop APIs for system to communicate with other systems #6 — Payroll Manager: As a payroll manager, I would like to integrate a payroll processing API to our HRIS to automate data transfer and reduce human error.
Document Employee Learning Performance Improvement Recommendation #11 - As an employee of GRU, I would like to see how far my progress is in completing the learning material, so I can know much I have left.

Sprint 1 — Selected User Stories

Nine user stories were pulled into Sprint 1. Each followed proper "As a [role], I want [goal], so that [benefit]" framing with explicit, testable acceptance criteria — not vague feature descriptions.

# Story Sample Acceptance Criteria
5 HR Manager — SSO: access multiple systems without repeated logins Role-based access; intuitive design; no repeated credential entry
6 Payroll Manager — Integration: automate payroll API data transfer Data transfer automated; error rate below human baseline; fully legally compliant
11 Employee — Completion Progress: track own learning progress See assignments remaining; view finished assignments; get notified of new ones
12 HR Manager — Employee Progress: track employee learning company-wide See assignments remaining and % completion per employee
47 Employee — Minimal System Downtime: reliable 24/7 access to pay/performance data 99.9% availability; downtime under 1.45 min/day; 5-hour advance notice; access denied gracefully during downtime
48 Legal Officer — Scheduled Downtime Alerts: advance notice to stay compliant 5-hr notice for maintenance, 24-hr for non-maintenance; support contact available during downtime

Story #47 is a good example of the backlog doing real work: it takes the abstract NFR target from the Vision & Scope doc ("three nines availability") and turns it into a concrete, testable condition — under 1.45 minutes of downtime per day, with a defined advance-notice window. Stories #11 and #12 are also a nice pair — the same underlying feature (learning progress tracking) written twice, once from the employee's perspective and once from the manager's, which is good practice for making sure a feature actually serves both ends of a relationship rather than just one.

Rollout & Adoption

Because GRU employees were already familiar with most of the underlying systems being integrated, we designed the rollout to minimize disruption rather than retrain from scratch. In-app tutorials would target only the genuinely new functionality — performance management modules for managers, compensation-criteria tools for HR, and new reporting tools for the Payroll Manager — rather than re-teaching the whole system. The update would also be announced well ahead of launch, with a support team and documentation in place at go-live.

Closing

EquiCare never left the documentation and wireframing stage — there was no working prototype to test with real users, and no opportunity to validate whether the Comparison Tool's disparity calculations would actually change a manager's behavior in practice. That's a real limitation, and I think it's more useful to name it directly than to imply otherwise. What this project did teach me is how much of product work happens before a single screen gets built — translating a vague organizational concern ("we might have pay disparities") into specific user stories, measurable non-functional requirements, and a permission model that actually reflects who should see what. That discipline is different from, but just as necessary as, the iterative testing work I did on DawgRide.
