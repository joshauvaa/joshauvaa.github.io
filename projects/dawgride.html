A companion mobile app that fixes the trust and usability gaps in UW's late-night shuttle services — built on top of Husky NightRide and SafeTrip, not as a replacement for them.

Context · Hypothesis · Market · Audience · Insights

Context

UW's late-night transportation safety net — Husky NightRide and SafeTrip — already exists, but students don't trust or enjoy using it. Our team set out to design a companion app, DawgRide, that sits on top of these existing services and fixes the experience layer: visibility, trust, and ease of use — without needing to rebuild UW's underlying transit infrastructure.

This wasn't a problem we invented for a class project. Student frustration with NightRide is publicly documented in UW's own student newspaper, which gave us a real foundation to design against rather than guessing at pain points.

Hypothesis

If we give UW students accurate, real-time visibility into NightRide and SafeTrip — instead of relying on a fixed schedule and an outdated standalone app — then they will feel safer and more confident planning late-night commutes, increasing trust in and usage of these existing services.

The Audience

Our target audience is UW students and staff who use Husky NightRide and SafeTrip, particularly those who are out on campus late — studying, working, or commuting home after dark. We narrowed this further into two personas to ground our design decisions in specific, different needs rather than a single generic "student" user.

User Insights

Our initial pain points were grounded in a real, published source rather than assumption — The Daily, UW's student newspaper, documented specific complaints about NightRide that mapped directly onto what we designed against:

  • Lateness — NightRide shuttles frequently deviate from their fixed route, making arrival times unpredictable.
  • Unintuitive mobile app — The existing tool, RideSystems, has an outdated interface that makes basic tasks harder than they should be.
  • Low discoverability — Information about NightRide and SafeTrip lives only on the UW website, spread across multiple nested pages, instead of being available in the moment a student actually needs it.

The template also calls for direct user quotes or survey stats at this stage. Our deck doesn't capture verbatim quotes from this early discovery phase — our actual interview data comes later, during user testing of the prototype (see Develop phase).

User Journey · Takeaways · Problem · Goal

User Journey

We mapped the current-state experience for each persona — tracing every step they'd take trying to use NightRide or SafeTrip today, with their expectations and frustrations annotated along the way. Both journeys surface the same core failure: the information layer breaks down right when users need it most.

George's Journey — NightRide
George plans his first NightRide trip around closing time — only to wait 10+ minutes past the scheduled arrival with no way to track the shuttle, ultimately ending up far from his dorm.
Devin's Journey — SafeTrip
Devin uses SafeTrip after a late study session — navigating a phone call, waiting outside alone for 12 minutes with no tracking, and resolving to ask for an ETA next time.

Big Takeaways

Combining our user insights, the core takeaway was that NightRide and SafeTrip's problems aren't really about the underlying transit service — the shuttles exist and the safety program exists. The failure is entirely in the information layer: students can't see what's happening, can't trust an ETA, and can't easily find how to use the services in the first place. That reframing is what shaped our solution — we weren't trying to fix transit logistics, we were trying to fix visibility and trust.

The Problem

UW students experience uncertainty and frustration when using Husky NightRide and SafeTrip at night. The lack of accurate, real-time updates on ride availability and queue position disrupts their ability to plan safe, efficient commutes.

The Goal

Success means students feel confident and informed enough to actually rely on NightRide and SafeTrip late at night, rather than avoiding them or defaulting to walking alone. From the user's perspective, that looks like knowing how long a wait will be and where to go. From a program standpoint, it looks like increased trust in — and usage of — services UW has already built and funded.

We didn't define a specific quantitative success target (e.g. "X% reduction in self-reported wait anxiety" or "X% increase in NightRide usage") as part of this coursework project — see Measuring Success below, where we define what we'd track if this were a real launch.

Positioning Statement

For UW students and staff who use the NightRide and SafeTrip services, DawgRide is a mobile app that helps students plan their commutes better. Unlike RideSystems, our product gives students accurate, real-time updates that help them feel safer commuting from campus at night.

Prioritization · Solutions · Testing · Final Design

Feature Prioritization & MVP Definition

We didn't run a formal RICE score against every feature idea during this project — our prioritization was driven directly by user testing signal, which led to a real pivot (see below). For the purpose of this case study, here's a RICE-style view of our core features, scored retroactively based on what we now know from testing:

Feature (User Story) Reach Impact Effort Confidence Total
"As a student waiting at night, I want to see real-time shuttle location so that I know how long until pickup." 9 9 8 6 ~7.6
"As a student new to NightRide, I want a simple, guided way to request a ride so that I'm not confused by the existing app." 8 8 4 9 ~14.4
"As a student walking alone, I want to report or check unsafe areas so that I can avoid them." 6 7 5 7 ~8.4
"As a student deciding whether to wait, I want to see shuttle occupancy so that I know what to expect." 7 6 5 6 ~8.4

Scores above (1–10 scale, Total = Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort, simplified) are reconstructed for this case study to illustrate prioritization logic — they weren't calculated live during the original project. What is real: usability and onboarding ended up mattering more than live tracking once we tested with actual users, which the scores above reflect.

Solutions Explored

Our first instinct was that live GPS tracking — knowing exactly where the shuttle was in real time — would be the highest-value feature, since "where is my ride" was the loudest pain point in our research. We spent roughly two weeks pursuing this, including evaluating third-party APIs like OneBusAway, before testing it with real users.

User Testing & Feedback

We ran 7 user tests across two iterations of our prototype.

1

Iteration 1 — Figma Prototype

As PM, I designed the first clickable Figma prototype myself — every screen, flow, and interaction state — rather than dividing it across the team. With attendance starting to slip heading into this phase, a single, complete prototype meant the dev team always had one unambiguous source of truth to build against, regardless of who was available in a given week. This was the version we put in front of our first round of test participants and the direct basis for the React build that followed.

Onboarding
Splash screen, login, sign-up, and loading state — the full entry flow using UW NetID credentials.
NightRide
Interactive map with live shuttle positions, stop selector, route search, and an ETA + occupancy card — the core of what was missing from the existing RideSystems app.
SafeTrip
Service area map, student ID verification, simplified queue-join flow with estimated wait time, and saved drop-off locations — replacing the existing phone-call-only process.
Safety Reporting
Full report submission flow — ID verification, incident type (Cops Nearby / Unsafe Area), detail entry, and a live map showing submitted reports from other users.
FAQ & Info
Accordion FAQ covering both NightRide and SafeTrip — surfacing the service information that previously required digging through multiple UW website pages.
2

Iteration 2

Users needed a clearer way to undo actions (backtrack on a form); a tutorial/key for the map was needed to explain symbols and usage; the NightRide schedule needed to live inside the app, not just be referenced.

3

Iteration 3

The Safety tab needed to visibly update with newly submitted reports; color contrast on tabs and informational text needed improvement; error messages were needed for incorrect form inputs.

The most important insight from testing: participants were getting stuck on fundamental usability issues — filtering, navigation, knowing how to back out of a flow — well before live tracking would have even mattered to them. That's what triggered our pivot.

→

The Pivot

We deprioritized live GPS tracking and reallocated that time toward fixing core usability — making filtering more intuitive and fixing the ability to cleanly cancel out of the NightRide section. A flashier feature isn't worth shipping if users are still getting stuck on the basics underneath it.

Keeping the Team on Track

Attendance held up well for the first three weeks, then got inconsistent for several members through weeks four to seven — the exact stretch where we needed to move from concept to a working, testable build. Some of that was unavoidable; a lot of it was last-minute. Either way, the deadline didn't move, so the plan had to absorb it.

As PM, I managed this on three fronts at once: I reassigned tasks from members who couldn't reliably show up to teammates who could, so work in flight didn't stall on any one person's schedule. Where reassignment wasn't enough, I picked up pieces myself — including owning the Figma prototype end-to-end rather than splitting it across a team whose availability I couldn't predict week to week. And where neither of those covered the gap, I cut scope: the live-tracking pivot above wasn't only a response to user testing data, it was also a practical call about what we could still deliver well with the team capacity we actually had, versus what we'd originally hoped to build.

The lesson that stuck with me: protecting a deadline with an inconsistent team isn't one decision, it's a running judgment call — knowing when to redistribute, when to absorb the work yourself, and when to cut scope instead of quietly letting quality slip.

Final Solution

DawgRide enhances the safety and convenience of late-night commutes for the UW community by building on top of the existing NightRide and SafeTrip services, rather than replacing them.

  • Interactive Map — shuttle routes and stops, for better trip planning
  • Real-Time Tracking — live shuttle locations for accurate arrival estimates
  • Shuttle Occupancy — visibility into how full a NightRide shuttle is before it arrives
  • SafeTrip Queue — a simplified flow for requesting a SafeTrip ride
  • Safety Map — lets users report and review suspicious activity on campus
  • Info/FAQ Section — explains how NightRide, SafeTrip, and DawgRide work together

Launch · Metrics · Future · Closing

Launch & GTM Strategy

Gap — not in original deck As a coursework MVP, DawgRide was never actually launched to real users at scale, so we didn't define a formal go-to-market or rollout plan (A/B testing, phased release, etc.). If this were carried forward as a real UW initiative, the natural approach — mirroring how Anthony Saltarelli's PayPal case study handled this — would be a small phased rollout: pilot with a single residence hall or a specific NightRide route first, gather usage data, then expand campus-wide.

Measuring Success

We didn't define formal North Star or leading/lagging metrics during the original coursework project. Below is what we'd define if DawgRide moved toward a real launch:

★

North Star Metric

Weekly active NightRide/SafeTrip requests initiated through DawgRide.

↑

Leading Indicators

App opens before 11pm; search/filter usage rate; Safety Map report submissions.

⏱

Lagging Indicators

Week-over-week growth in unique riders using DawgRide to request NightRide or SafeTrip; reduction in support/complaint tickets about wait uncertainty.

⚠

Counter Metrics

Drop-off rate between opening the app and completing a request (would signal the new flow adds friction rather than removing it); false/spam Safety Map reports.

◎

Monitoring

Would require coordination with UW Transportation Services and UW Police (who operate SafeTrip) to validate report accuracy and catch service-level issues early.

Future Iterations

  • A client-side interface for NightRide/SafeTrip drivers to report their own vehicle locations — the real dependency live tracking would need
  • A mapping API integration for in-app turn-by-turn directions
  • A formal queue system for SafeTrip requests
  • 48-hour storage and more comprehensive identification handling for safety reports
  • User verification through UW NetID
  • Real vehicle integration into the live application

Closing

We started with a clear, well-documented problem: UW students don't trust or enjoy using NightRide and SafeTrip, not because the services are bad, but because the information layer around them is broken. We built DawgRide to fix that layer. Along the way, the most important lesson wasn't technical — it was that our instinct about what mattered most (live tracking) didn't match what users actually struggled with (basic usability), and we had to be willing to redirect real effort, mid-project, once the data told us otherwise.

Leading that pivot meant more than reading the test results correctly. It meant keeping a team with uneven availability aligned and accountable under a real deadline — deciding week to week whether to redistribute work, absorb it myself, or cut scope outright. That experience taught me that prioritization, as a PM, isn't a single decision made once at kickoff. It's a discipline you keep exercising as conditions change underneath you.
