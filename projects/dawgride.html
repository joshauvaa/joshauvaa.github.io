DawgRide

A companion mobile app that fixes the trust and usability gaps in UW's late-night shuttle services.

My Role PM/Frontend Developer
Team 3 Frontend developers , 2 Backend Developers
Context Cooperative Software Development Course - 10 weeks (Jan 2025 - Mar 2025)
Tools HTML/CSS, JavaScript, OpenStreetMap API
Executive Summary

Challenge: UW students lacked confidence in Husky NightRide and SafeTrip because of poor visibility into ride status, confusing service information, and uncertain wait times.

Role: Product Manager & Frontend Developer for a five-person team. I led product discovery, MVP definition, prototype design, usability testing, scope prioritization, and frontend implementation.

Key Decisions: Defined an MVP around validating user value instead of technical completeness, then reprioritized the roadmap after usability testing challenged our original assumptions.

Outcome: Delivered a functional MVP and learned how product decisions evolve through user evidence, technical constraints, and delivery realities.

1

Discover

Context · Hypothesis · Market · Audience · Insights

Context

UW's late-night transportation services (Husky NightRide and SafeTrip) already exist, but students don't trust or enjoy using it. Our team set out to design a companion app, DawgRide, that sits on top of these existing services and fixes the experience layer, emphasizing visibility, trust, and ease of use.

Hypothesis

If we give UW students accurate, real-time visibility into NightRide and SafeTrip — instead of relying on a fixed schedule and an outdated standalone app — then they will feel safer and more confident planning late-night commutes, increasing trust in and usage of these existing services.

The Market

DawgRide wasn't designed to replace NightRide or SafeTrip — it was designed to fix the experience layer on top of them. Understanding what each service actually offered and their shortcomings was the starting point for scoping what DawgRide needed to do.

Husky NightRide
What it is Free fare shuttle service running fixed routes around campus within East or West zone boundaries
Hours 8 pm – ~1:30 am Mon–Fri
How to use Show up at one of 8 scheduled stops. Track via RideSystems app (iOS/Android) or SMS
The gap RideSystems has an outdated interface and unintuitive UX
Husky SafeTrip
What it is On-demand vehicle escort with a uniformed UWPD security guard.
Hours 6:30 pm – 2 am daily
How to use Call 206-685-9255. Tell the dispatcher your location, wait outside near the entryway, watch for the security guard car
The gap No app, no form, no way to book without a phone call, and no way to track your ride after you've called

Both services are genuinely useful — the problem isn't what they offer, it's how hard they are to access and monitor. DawgRide's job was to make the information layer around both services as reliable as the services themselves.

The Audience

Our target audience included UW students and staff who use Husky NightRide and SafeTrip, particularly those who are out on campus late — studying, working, or commuting home after dark. These two personas grounded our design decisions and served as a reference point throughout the project.

User Insights

Our initial pain points were identified through a written piece from The Daily, UW's student newspaper.

  • Lateness — NightRide shuttles frequently deviate from their fixed route, making arrival times unpredictable.
  • Unintuitive mobile app — The existing tool, RideSystems, has an outdated interface that makes basic tasks harder than they should be.
  • Low discoverability — Information about NightRide and SafeTrip lives only on the UW website, spread across multiple nested pages.

Define

User Journey · Takeaways · Problem · Goal

User Journey

These user journeys mapped our personas' experience using NightRide and SafeTrip in the current state. Both journeys surface the same core failure: the information layer breaks down right when users need it most.

George's Journey — NightRide
George plans his first NightRide trip around closing time — only to wait 10+ minutes past the scheduled arrival with no way to track the shuttle, ultimately ending up far from his dorm.
Devin's Journey — SafeTrip
Devin uses SafeTrip after a late study session — navigating a phone call, waiting outside alone for 12 minutes with no tracking, and resolving to ask for an ETA next time.

Big Takeaways

Combining our user insights, the core takeaway was that NightRide and SafeTrip's problems aren't really about the underlying transit service. The failure lies in the information layer. Students can't see track their ride, can't trust an ETA, and can't easily find how to use the services in the first place. This reframing shaped our solution, which would need to centralize visibility and trust.

The Problem

UW students experience uncertainty and frustration when using Husky NightRide and SafeTrip at night. The lack of accurate, real-time updates on ride availability and queue position disrupts their ability to plan safe, efficient commutes.

The Goal

Success means students feel confident and informed enough to actually rely on NightRide and SafeTrip late at night, rather than avoiding them or defaulting to walking alone. From the user's perspective, that looks like knowing how long a wait will be and where to go. From a service's standpoint, it looks like increased trust in, and usage of the services UW has already built and funded.

Positioning Statement

For UW students and staff who use the NightRide and SafeTrip services, DawgRide is a mobile app that helps students plan their commutes better. Unlike RideSystems, our product gives students accurate, real-time updates that help them feel safer commuting from campus at night.

Develop

Prioritization · Solutions · Testing · Final Design

Feature Prioritization & MVP Definition

One of the most valuable product discussions during DawgRide wasn't deciding which feature to build first - it was defining what qualified as a true Minimum Viable Product.

From the beginning, our vision centered around real-time shuttle tracking. Students consistently cited uncertainty around shuttle arrival as a major pain point, making live tracking feel like the defining feature of the product.

However, implementing true live tracking presented a daunting challenge. We explored publicly available UW transportation data, investigated whether existing APIs exposed vehicle locations, and even discussed temporary hardware-based tracking approaches during development. None appeared to be feasible within our ten-week timeline.

◆

Defining the MVP

Decision: Instead of delaying the project while waiting for unavailable infrastructure, we simulated shuttle movement using predetermined routes and timed progression.

Reasoning: This allowed us to validate the user experience without depending on data that was limited or out of scope.

Key Lesson: An MVP isn't the smallest version of the final product. It's the smallest version capable of testing your most important assumptions.

Solutions Explored

Our first instinct was that live GPS tracking — knowing exactly where the shuttle was in real time — would be the highest-value feature, since "where is my ride" was the loudest pain point in our research. We spent roughly two weeks pursuing this, including evaluating third-party APIs like OneBusAway, before testing it with real users.

Usability Testing & Product Decisions

We ran 10 user tests across two iterations of our prototype.

1

Iteration 1 — Figma Prototype

We created a high-fidelity interactive prototype simulating the complete experience for both services to validate our assumptions before committing development effort.The prototype served as the foundation for our first round of usability testing and allowed us to evaluate user behavior, identify friction points, and iterate before implementation began.

Onboarding
Splash screen, login, sign-up, and loading state — the full entry flow using UW NetID credentials.
NightRide
Interactive map with live shuttle positions, stop selector, route search, and an ETA + occupancy card — the core of what was missing from the existing RideSystems app.
SafeTrip
Service area map, student ID verification, simplified queue-join flow with estimated wait time, and saved drop-off locations — replacing the existing phone-call-only process.
Safety Reporting
Full report submission flow — ID verification, incident type (Cops Nearby / Unsafe Area), detail entry, and a live map showing submitted reports from other users.
FAQ & Info
Accordion FAQ covering both NightRide and SafeTrip — surfacing the service information that previously required digging through multiple UW website pages.
2

Iteration 2 Feedback

Users needed a clearer way to undo actions (backtrack on a form); a tutorial/key for the map was needed to explain symbols and usage; the NightRide schedule needed to live inside the app, not just be referenced.

The most important insight from testing: participants were getting stuck on fundamental usability issues (filtering, navigation, knowing how to back out of a flow) well before live tracking would have even mattered to them. That's what triggered our pivot.

→

Key Product Decision

We deprioritized live GPS tracking and reallocated that time toward fixing core usability, streamlining the flow of tracking a shuttle along with reverse navigation for both sections. A flashier feature isn't worth shipping if users are still getting stuck on basic use cases.

Managing Delivery Under Constraints

Attendance held up well for the first three weeks, then got inconsistent for several members through weeks four to seven - arguably the most critical period of the project. Some of that was unavoidable, though a lot of it was last-minute. Either way, the deadline didn't move, so the plan had to absorb it.

As PM, I managed this on three fronts. I led efforts to redelegate tasks from members who couldn't reliably show up to more active counterparts to keep the project moving forward. Where reassignment wasn't enough, I picked up pieces myself — including owning the Figma prototype end-to-end rather than splitting it across a team whose availability I couldn't predict week to week. And where neither of those covered the gap, I called out the need to cut scope. The live-tracking pivot above wasn't only a response to user testing data, it was also a practical call about what we could still deliver well with the team capacity we actually had, versus what we'd originally hoped to build.

My biggest takeaway was that managing a deadline in a dynamic product environment means continuously adapting as priorities, scope, and team capacity change. Understanding each team member's skillset, reallocating work when needed, and making thoughtful tradeoffs without losing sight of the project's core goals are going to be vital when executing future projects.

Final Solution

DawgRide enhances the safety and convenience of late-night commutes for the UW community by building on top of the existing NightRide and SafeTrip services, rather than replacing them.

  • Interactive Map — shuttle routes and stops, for better trip planning
  • Real-Time Tracking — live shuttle locations for accurate arrival estimates
  • Shuttle Occupancy — visibility into how full a NightRide shuttle is before it arrives
  • SafeTrip Queue — a simplified flow for requesting a SafeTrip ride
  • Safety Map — lets users report and review suspicious activity on campus
  • Info/FAQ Section — explains how NightRide, SafeTrip, and DawgRide work together

Deliver

Launch · Metrics · Future · Closing

Launch & GTM Strategy

As a coursework MVP, DawgRide was never actually launched to real users at scale, so we didn't define a formal go-to-market or rollout plan (A/B testing, phased release, etc.). If this were carried forward as a real UW initiative, the natural approach would be a small phased rollout: pilot with a single focus group, gather usage data, then expand campus-wide.

Measuring Success

We didn't define formal North Star or leading/lagging metrics as part of the course. Below is what I'd define if DawgRide moved toward a real launch:

★

North Star Metric

Weekly active NightRide/SafeTrip requests initiated through DawgRide.

↑

Leading Indicators

App opens before 11pm; search/filter usage rate; Safety Map report submissions.

⏱

Lagging Indicators

Week-over-week growth in unique riders using DawgRide to request NightRide or SafeTrip; reduction in support/complaint tickets about wait uncertainty.

⚠

Counter Metrics

Drop-off rate between opening the app and completing a request (would signal the new flow adds friction rather than removing it); false/spam Safety Map reports.

◎

Monitoring

Would require coordination with UW Transportation Services and UW Police (who operate SafeTrip) to validate report accuracy and catch service-level issues early.

Future Iterations

  • A client-side interface for NightRide/SafeTrip drivers to report their own vehicle locations
  • A mapping API integration for in-app turn-by-turn directions
  • A formal queue system for SafeTrip requests
  • 48-hour storage and more comprehensive identification handling for safety reports
  • User verification through UW NetID
  • Real vehicle integration into the live application

Closing

We started with a clear, well-documented problem: UW students don't trust or enjoy using NightRide and SafeTrip, not because the services are bad, but because the information layer around them is broken. We built DawgRide to fix that layer. Along the way, the most important lesson wasn't technical. It was that our instinct about what mattered most (live tracking) didn't match what users actually struggled with (basic usability), and we had to be willing to redirect real effort, mid-project, once the data told us otherwise.

Leading that pivot meant more than reading the test results correctly. It meant keeping a team with uneven availability aligned and accountable under a real deadline. That experience taught me that prioritization, as a PM, isn't a single decision made once at kickoff. It's something you'll consistently revisit as circumstnaces naturally evolve.
